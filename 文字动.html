My name is Ah Fei, born in Heluo. As a child, my favorite was the fragrance of mugwort and mint leaves when making Lei Cha.

I remember that day, my grandfather's hands were still wet, holding the bowl, tea and sweat intertwined, swirling in the bowl, the ripples rising like our constantly migrating fate.

Lei Cha is more than just tea.

Our ancestors lived in Bianzhou in the Central Plains for generations, the most fertile land by the Yellow River.

Grandfather said we were not originally "Hakka people," we were just refugees.

We traveled south, stopping and going along the way, crossing the Yangtze River, climbing over Meiling, traversing the old forests of Gannan, walking through poisonous swamps and miasma.

Some fellow villagers settled locally, while others left us. The land and language here did not belong to us, we were guests here.

Later, Uncle decided to go to the Golden Mountain across the ocean.

To make himself let go, he even smashed the Lei Cha bowls at home.

Dear Mother:

I have arrived in San Francisco, the work here is hard, but I will persevere.

I hope this letter can tell you: I am still here, I am still alive.

Your unfilial son

That was Uncle's first time on a ship, standing on the deck, the taste of Lei Cha getting further and further away.

When he arrived in San Francisco, he was taken from the dock to build railroads. White supervisors whipped them with whips, calling them "cheap livestock."

Some died, buried under the tracks, without tombstones, without names, like dust scattered by the wind.

We built our first enclosed house in Fujian, stacking mud bricks one by one, like building our own roots.

Inside the enclosed house we spoke Hakka, outside the walls we spoke Cantonese, two worlds we did not want, and could not imagine meeting.

Some laughed at our heavy accents, calling us "strange" behind our backs; some said we were aliens, not eating local food.

So we invented food that suited our own taste: Lei Cha.

In 1981, the wave of reform and opening up spread across China, and my grandparents also seized this opportunity to leave the mountains where they had lived for over 40 years.

Those mountains that had sheltered, but also trapped many Hakka people. They walked hundreds of kilometers on foot to the Pearl River Delta to seek their fortune.
